Current Role

Precinct Captain.

Elected Democratic Precinct Captain serving Chicago's 48th Ward. Responsible for voter outreach, community organizing, and connecting constituents to local government.

What a Precinct Captain does: The precinct captain is the most local level of political organization in Chicago — responsible for a specific geographic precinct within the ward. The role involves voter registration and turnout, door-to-door canvassing, connecting residents to aldermanic services, and serving as the bridge between the community and city government.

Why it matters: Local politics is where policy actually touches people's lives — streets, schools, sanitation, zoning, public safety. A precinct captain who can organize effectively and leverage technology can make city services more accessible and responsive to the people who need them.

The approach: The same production discipline that runs through enterprise AI work applies here — systems thinking, measurable outcomes, technology that serves rather than replaces human relationships. Civic tech should make organizing easier, not replace the door-knocking and one-on-one conversations that actually build community.

Responsibilities

  • Voter Outreach — Door-to-door canvassing, voter registration drives, election day turnout operations for the precinct.
  • Constituent Services — Connecting residents to the alderman's office for city services: potholes, streetlights, permits, sanitation, zoning questions.
  • Community Organizing — Building relationships across the precinct, identifying community concerns, and advocating for neighborhood priorities.
  • Civic Intelligence — Leveraging technology (Your48, Leni48) to make local government more accessible and organizing more effective.

Philosophy

Technology that serves.

Civic tech should make organizing easier, not replace the relationships that actually build community.

Principle
Relationships First

Human Infrastructure

Technology supports door-knocking, not replaces it. The goal is to make the human work of community organizing more effective — tracking conversations, surfacing issues, coordinating volunteers — not to automate away the relationships that build trust.

Principle
Accessible by Default

Civic Access

City services shouldn't require knowing the right person or navigating a maze of phone trees. Civic tech should make local government as accessible as any consumer app — without losing the human touch when it matters.

Principle
Measurable Impact

Production Discipline

The same rigor that goes into enterprise systems applies here. Every platform should have clear metrics — service requests fulfilled, voters contacted, turnout improvement — because public service deserves the same accountability as commercial work.

Principle
Local Knowledge

Community Intelligence

The best civic tech surfaces what the community already knows — which blocks have which issues, who the trusted voices are, what's worked before — rather than imposing outside solutions on local problems.

The 48th Ward

Edgewater. Chicago.

Chicago's 48th Ward covers Edgewater, Andersonville, and parts of Rogers Park — one of the most diverse wards in the city.

The 48th Ward stretches along the lakefront on Chicago's far north side, including the neighborhoods of Edgewater, Andersonville, and parts of Rogers Park. It's one of the most ethnically, economically, and culturally diverse wards in the city — home to immigrants from across the world, longtime Chicago families, young professionals, artists, and retirees.

What makes it unique: The 48th has always been a political bellwether — diverse enough to reflect the broader city, engaged enough to turn out in high numbers, and progressive enough to push the city forward on housing, transit, and public safety. It's also home to some of Chicago's best neighborhood institutions: Andersonville's Swedish heritage, Edgewater's lakefront parks, and a thriving small-business corridor along Clark Street.

Current alderman: The ward is currently represented by Alderman Leni Manaa-Hoppenworth, who took office in 2023 with a mandate for affordable housing, public safety reform, and constituent services.

Ward Facts

  • Neighborhoods: Edgewater, Andersonville, parts of Rogers Park
  • Population: ~55,000 residents
  • Precincts: 60+ voting precincts
  • Character: Lakefront, diverse, politically engaged
  • Current Alderman: Leni Manaa-Hoppenworth (2023–present)
